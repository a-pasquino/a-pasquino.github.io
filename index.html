0. Introduction¶

Video games are a medium that has taken the world by storm over the few decades they have existed. From a niche industry in the early 1980s to one that generates on the order of $100B annually, the growth has provided a relatively new industry to inspect. Many people less familiar with the games industry would first think of home consoles such as the Xbox and PlayStation, but a significant portion of the industry lives and thrives through computer games.

The undisputed titan of the PC gaming marketplace is Steam a storefront and online hub established in 2003 by game development studio/publisher Valve. It changed the landscape of online gaming, essentially acting as the Amazon.com for PC games. Most major studios list their games to be bought and played through Steam, and its user profile and friend system provide a social network surrogate on top of its marketplace function.

Steam is host to tens of thousands of games and millions of users, which means, as no surprise to anyone who's aware that this is a data analysis document, there is plenty of information! In this tutorial, we'll be taking a look at trends in game popularity and examining what keeps the platform ticking.

2a. Auxiliary Tables¶

Now that we have our data, we want it to be in a format that is easy for our code to analyze. As text isn't particularly easy to do calculations on, we can convert some of our text fields into numerical data. To do this, we will one-hot encode a few of our columns of string lists into separate tables of binary information. The tags table already follows this format and has a workable form of the steamspy_tags column, so we will let that column alone and work on the other three dimensions we want to use: genres, categories, and platforms.

def clean_name(df):
  # special characters 
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.replace('[\N{COPYRIGHT SIGN}\N{TRADE MARK SIGN}\N{REGISTERED SIGN}]', '')
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.replace(':', '')
  # the opposite of edition is subtraction
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.replace('- Game of the Year Edition', '')
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.replace(' Game of the Year Edition', '')
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.replace(' Game of the Year', '')
  # whitespace stragglers
  df['name'] = df['name'].str.strip()

3b. "Quality"¶

As we can see with the discrepancy between ownership and playtime, just because games are being bought does not mean they're being played. With thousands of games being published each year and only a few making headlines, it makes sense that many games slip through the cracks. To evaluate what's being played, we can look at the average playtimes of games - that is, the hours played per owner.

Another piece of information we can utilize to gauge the quality of a game is the user ratings. We can pull that information back in from our games table, do a quick calculation, and graph it against the average playtime.

Unsurprisingly, people do not tend to spend hundreds of hours on games they don't like very much, resulting in an empty lower right segment of this graph. We can see that most games are clustered around low playtime. This includes the majority of single-player games, as they tend to have more limited capacity for play. The darker point in the top left is Counter-Strike: Global Offensive, one of the largest competitive FPS games on the platform. Competitive games like this tend to skew higher in playtime due to their endless nature.

Another thing of note is the density of games toward the top of the graph. Most games are liked, at least on paper. This could be the result of a tendency for proponents of a game being more likely to leave a rating than those who disliked it, or people being choosy about what they play, since Steam only allows users to rate games they've played.

3c. Complexity¶

Another metric that can be evaluated is the complexity of a game. This is a bit more difficult to evaluate numerically, but we can develop a metric for it. For our purposes, we will use the sum of a game's tags - the more tags, the more bases a game covers, and the more complex it is.

In [97]:
# sum each row's tags for a complexity rating
df_tags = tags[['appid']]
df_tags['tag_sum'] = tags.drop(columns=['appid']).sum(axis='columns')
df_complexity = df.merge(df_tags, on=['appid'])
df_complexity.head()

plt.ylim(0,100)

plt.grid()

It seems as though more complex games are more highly rated, and the only games doing poorly are those that are very simple. This is an interesting discovery, as it can open more questions about what gamers find valuable in a game. Does the addition of more game mechanics and avenues of expression equal a better game? And if this correlation truly exists, are developers striving to meet this demand for complex games? We can answer this second question by taking a look at complexity over time.

4. Conclusion¶

From our exploration, we have gained some interesting insights into behaviors of Steam users, as well as opened a few doors to more possible questions to ask about this, and associated, data. A data with more granular timeseries information could allow for a breakdown of day-to-day or month-to-month play habits of the user base. This could also allow for explorations of popularity of different tags or genres at different points in time, not just release trends. Additionally, this doesn't even begin to look at information about specific games, or even groups of games within a genre or tag.

Steam is a large platform with tons of available data that other researchers have pulled. This is mostly a bird's-eye view of this corner of PC gaming that aims to act as a foot in the door for anyone interested in statistics.

Data is available at the links in the introduction or here on my github page. Thanks for reading!
